<p>Notice that most threats are about <strong>identity and device posture</strong> — not exotic malware. This is exactly why Zero Trust exists.</p>
<h2>Zero Trust: What It Actually Means in Practice</h2>
<p>The certification teaches you the definition: <em>"Never trust, always verify."</em></p>
<p>The practice looks like this:</p>
<h3>Step 1: Every Device Must Be Enrolled and Compliant</h3>
<p>Using Microsoft Intune, we enforced that no device could access corporate resources unless:</p>
<ul>
<li>Enrolled in Intune/Azure AD (Entra ID)</li>
<li>Running approved OS version</li>
<li>Encryption enabled (BitLocker / FileVault)</li>
<li>Antivirus definition current (Defender / Trellix)</li>
</ul>
<pre><code># Intune Compliance Policy — Minimum Requirements
OS: Windows 11 22H2+ or macOS 13+
Disk encryption: Required
Firewall: Required  
Defender: Real-time protection ON
Password: Required, min 12 chars, complexity ON
</code></pre>
<p>Non-compliant devices got <strong>Conditional Access blocks</strong> — they could see the sign-in page but couldn't authenticate until they fixed compliance.</p>
<h3>Step 2: MFA Everywhere (But Not Annoying)</h3>
<p>The classic mistake is enabling MFA and then getting flooded with complaints. The right approach:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Named locations</strong> (corporate office IP ranges) = trusted, no MFA required</li>
<li><strong>Unknown location + sensitive app</strong> = MFA required</li>
<li><strong>New device + any location</strong> = MFA + device registration required</li>
<li><strong>Executive accounts</strong> = FIDO2 security key (no SMS/authenticator)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Step 3: Privileged Access — Minimum Viable Permissions</h3>
<p>Every IT admin account had only the permissions needed for their specific role. No permanent Global Admin. Privileged Identity Management (PIM) for time-bound elevated access with audit logs.</p>
<h2>Conditional Access — The Most Powerful Tool You're Probably Underusing</h2>
<p>Here's a real policy set that protected a few hundred users at a multinational enterprise:</p>

<h2>What the Certification Exam Won't Tell You</h2>
<p><strong>1. Conditional Access conflicts are real.</strong> If you have 15 CA policies, the wrong combination will lock users out on a Friday evening. Always test in report-only mode first for 2 weeks.</p>
<p><strong>2. Named locations are your best friend.</strong> Get your office IP ranges documented before you deploy anything. The number of support calls drops dramatically when trusted locations are properly configured.</p>
<p><strong>3. Intune rollout requires change management, not just technology.</strong> Users react badly to suddenly having their personal phone enrolled in MDM. Communication, FAQ documents, and a soft launch period matter.</p>
<p><strong>4. Defender for M365 Secure Score is addictive.</strong> Once you start improving your score, you can't stop. We went from 41% to 78% in 6 months.</p>

<h2>The Human Layer Is Still the Weakest</h2>
<p>After all the technical controls, the most effective security improvement I made was a 30-minute awareness session with each new hire. Not a 2-hour compliance video — a real conversation about:</p>
<ul>
<li>What phishing actually looks like (real examples from corporate environments)</li>
<li>How to report a suspicious email (one click, one button)</li>
<li>What happens if they click something they shouldn't (no blame, just report)</li>
</ul>
<p>Humans are the firewall that Intune can't configure. Invest in them.</p>
