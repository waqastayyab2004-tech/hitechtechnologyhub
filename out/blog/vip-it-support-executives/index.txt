<p>That model fails catastrophically for C-level executives.</p>

<h2>The 5 Rules of Executive IT Support</h2>
<h3>Rule 1: Never Make Them Wait</h3>
<p>A senior executive with a laptop issue 10 minutes before a board call needs resolution in 5 minutes, not acknowledgment in 30. This means:</p>
<ul>
<li>Having a spare ready-to-go device for every executive</li>
<li>Knowing their device profile by heart (which laptop, which apps, which configurations)</li>
<li>Having their accounts pre-cached on a backup device</li>
</ul>
<p>I maintained a "hot spare" kit: one configured laptop, one configured iPhone, always charged, always enrolled, always ready.</p>
<h3>Rule 2: Anticipate, Don't React</h3>
<p>Before every major executive event, I conducted a pre-event IT check:</p>
<ul>
<li>Test all meeting room systems 2 hours before (not 5 minutes)</li>
<li>Verify Teams/Zoom credentials in advance</li>
<li>Check presentation laptops and display adapters</li>
<li>Confirm network connectivity and video conferencing quality</li>
<li>Have backup hotspot ready</li>
</ul>
<p>For major industry events and executive offsites, I arrived 3 hours early. In 5 years, I never had an AV failure during an executive event.</p>
<h3>Rule 3: Communicate Proactively — Even If You Have Nothing New</h3>
<p>Executives hate silence during incidents. If you're still working on an issue:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Update: I'm working on the issue. Estimated resolution in 20 minutes. I'll send you another message when it's resolved."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>That one sentence prevents three follow-up calls.</p>
<h3>Rule 4: Always Have a Workaround Ready</h3>
<p>The best IT engineers are not the ones who fix things fastest. They're the ones who always have an alternative path. Every major executive dependency needs a backup plan:</p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>Primary System</th>
<th>Backup Plan</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>Teams room system</td>
<td>Personal laptop + USB-C to HDMI</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Corporate laptop</td>
<td>Hot spare, pre-configured</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>VPN connection</td>
<td>Mobile hotspot + secondary VPN profile</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Corporate email</td>
<td>Webmail on personal device</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Video conference</td>
<td>Phone dial-in code</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<h3>Rule 5: Never Say "I Don't Know" — Say "I'll Find Out in 5 Minutes"</h3>
<p>Executives expect certainty. "I don't know" registers as incompetence, even if the question is genuinely complex.</p>
<p>"I'll find out in 5 minutes and come back to you" demonstrates that you're on it and gives you time to get the right answer. Always deliver on that 5-minute commitment.</p>
<h2>Managing High-Stakes Events</h2>
<p>The events I managed ranged from weekly office meetings to major industry conferences and international broadcasts with hundreds of attendees.</p>
<p>The event IT checklist template I developed:</p>
<pre><code>T-7 days: Confirm A/V vendor, venue network specs
T-3 days: Equipment delivery, test all systems end-to-end
T-1 day: Full rehearsal with presenter(s)
T-3 hours: Setup complete, system test
T-1 hour: Final check, presenter on-site walkthrough
T-15 min: All systems green, standby mode
During event: Dedicated IT presence, no phones (focus)
Post-event: Equipment pack-down, incident log if any issues
</code></pre>
<p>International broadcasts required coordinating with the global IT team across time zones — synchronized start times, dual network paths, and always a technical contact on both sides during the broadcast.</p>

<h2>The Metric Nobody Tracks But Should: Executive IT Satisfaction</h2>
<p>Standard CSAT surveys don't work for C-level. They don't have time to fill them out.</p>
<p>Instead, I tracked:</p>
<ul>
<li>Number of escalations that reached my manager (target: zero)</li>
<li>Number of events where IT was mentioned as a problem (target: zero)</li>
<li>Number of proactive solutions I delivered before being asked (track positives too)</li>
</ul>
<p>After 5 years of VIP support, I had zero event failures and zero escalations from the executive team. That's the metric.</p>
<h2>The Real Lesson</h2>
<p>VIP IT support is not about technical skills. It's about <em>trust</em>. Senior leadership needs to know that when they have a problem at 7 AM before a client presentation, you will answer the call and fix it.</p>
<p>Building that trust takes time, consistency, and an unfailing commitment to being available and reliable. The technical skills are necessary but not sufficient.</p>
<p>Be the person they never worry about. That's the highest achievement in executive IT support.</p>
