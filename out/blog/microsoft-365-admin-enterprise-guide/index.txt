<h2>The 10 Most Common M365 Issues (And How to Actually Fix Them)</h2>
<h3>1. "I Can't Access My Email From Outside the Office"</h3>
<p><strong>Cause 90% of the time:</strong> Conditional Access policy blocking non-compliant devices or non-trusted locations.</p>
<p><strong>Fix:</strong> Check the Sign-in Logs in Entra ID. Filter by user, look for the failed sign-in, expand the Conditional Access section. It will tell you <em>exactly</em> which policy blocked them and why.</p>
<h3>2. Email Keeps Going to Spam</h3>
<p>Check in this order:</p>
<ol>
<li>Exchange Quarantine — is it being held there?</li>
<li>Mail flow rules — has someone created an overly aggressive rule?</li>
<li>Anti-spam policy — sender domain reputation score</li>
<li>Check if the domain is on a blocklist: use <a href="https://mxtoolbox.com">MXToolbox</a></li>
</ol>

<h3>4. SharePoint "Access Denied"</h3>
<p>SharePoint permissions are the source of 40% of all M365 support tickets in a typical enterprise. The hierarchy:</p>
<pre><code>Tenant → Site Collection → Site → Library → Folder → File
</code></pre>
<p>Permissions cascade down, but <strong>broken inheritance</strong> breaks this. Always check:</p>
<ol>
<li>Does the user have a Site Collection license?</li>
<li>Was the sharing link expired?</li>
<li>Is there a sensitive label blocking external access?</li>
<li>Is the user in the correct Azure AD group?</li>
</ol>
<h3>5. OneDrive Sync Issues</h3>
<p>The most reliable fix:</p>
<pre><code>Stop sync → Sign out of OneDrive → Delete the local sync folder content → Re-link
</code></pre>
<p>But first: check the sync client version. Outdated OneDrive clients cause 60% of sync issues.</p>
<h2>Teams Governance: The Problem Nobody Addresses</h2>
<p>Without governance, Teams sprawl becomes an audit nightmare. After 6 months of no governance at one organisation, there were 47 active Teams channels — many abandoned, with sensitive files shared publicly.</p>
<p>The governance framework I implemented:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Teams creation policy</strong> — only IT admins and approved owners can create new Teams</li>
<li><strong>Guest access policy</strong> — external guests require IT manager approval</li>
<li><strong>Retention policy</strong> — Teams messages retained for 2 years, then archived</li>
<li><strong>Naming convention</strong> — all Teams prefixed with department code: <code>IT-</code>, <code>HR-</code></li>
<li><strong>Inactive Teams review</strong> — quarterly audit, archive if no activity for 90 days</li>
</ol>
<p>This reduced the Teams count from 47 to 22 active channels within one quarter.</p>

<h2>M365 Copilot: What I've Learned in the First 6 Months</h2>
<p>Enterprise deployments of M365 Copilot have been rolling out to select users. My observations:</p>
<p><strong>Where it delivers immediate value:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Summarising long email threads (10 min → 30 sec)</li>
<li>Transcribing and summarising Teams meeting recordings</li>
<li>Drafting first versions of routine communications</li>
<li>"Catch me up" on channels you missed</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Where it struggles:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Anything requiring deep domain knowledge without context</li>
<li>Generating accurate data — always verify numbers it produces</li>
<li>Following complex multi-step instructions reliably</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>The honest ROI number:</strong> Based on a pilot deployment, users with Copilot access saved an average of 45 minutes per day on communication tasks. That's meaningful — but it requires training users on <em>how</em> to use it, not just providing access.</p>
<h2>The M365 Admin Superpower: PowerShell</h2>
<p>Every M365 admin who relies only on the GUI is leaving 70% of their power unused. The tasks that take 10 minutes in the portal take 30 seconds in PowerShell:</p>
<pre><code class="language-powershell"># Export all users with last sign-in date (identify inactive accounts)
Connect-MgGraph -Scopes "User.Read.All", "AuditLog.Read.All"
Get-MgUser -All -Property "DisplayName,UserPrincipalName,SignInActivity" |
    Select DisplayName, UserPrincipalName, 
           @{N="LastSignIn";E={$_.SignInActivity.LastSignInDateTime}} |
    Where-Object { $_.LastSignIn -lt (Get-Date).AddDays(-90) } |
    Export-Csv "inactive_users.csv" -NoTypeInformation
</code></pre>
<p>Run this monthly. Any account with no sign-in in 90 days gets flagged for review — a key security hygiene step that prevents stale account risks.</p>
